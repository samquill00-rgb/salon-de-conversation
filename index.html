Présentation

Le Salon

'I wish you'd have been my tutor when I was young.'

'I wish I could be a fly-on-the-wall in your classes.'

There are many resons why people want to learn more.

The world is full of intellectuals without enough time.

— ✦ —

Perhaps there are children to care for, or a career which took them away from their academic studies (perhaps they are now retired). Perhaps they wish they had not studied Law, or English, or History, or Philosophy, but instead had studied History, Philosophy, Law, or English. Others still have spent years mastering a fiendish subject, and want only an intelligent peer to go with them, through the unfamiliar.

L’Esprit

We shall talk, talk, talk; we shall dispute and wonder.

I will bring you texts, old and new, some of which you might know well, or half-remember, and some of which will be entirely unfamiliar, drawn from the peculiar store of my years of study.

Most of all we will seek, together, to make sense of the world, as we find it now, in the context of the truth and beauty of things past.

Table choisie

Small-group salons focused on literature, philosophy, politics, art and history. We could think about how the things we study might speak to our modern hearts, or else how they shape the terrain of the 21st Century, in the broadest, most abstract terms, in geopolitics and philosophy. La spécialité de la maison

I will guide you through the literature, history, philosophy and theology of the Anglophone World (and beyond it, when that's required). The course begins with the earliest account of Britain (Pytheas of Massalia) and works onwards to the present, at your own pace.(though, of course, we can begin whenever you'd be most interested in beginning.)

Topics might include: post-Roman Anglo-Saxon poetry; the Viking invasion and the making of England; the tradition of courtly verse in Mediaeval Scots; Chaucer; the Arthurian legends; the Renaissance in various forms; early-modern drama (Shakespeare & Co.); the Reformation and the English Civil Wars (from both political and theological perspectives); John Milton, Thomas Hobbes and the 17th Century crisis of free speech; Neoclassicism; Jonathan Swift, Alexander Pope (their flaws and perfections); David Hume, radical conservatisim and the breakdown of the Enlightenment mind; the inventon of Gothic; pre-Romantic madness (Christopher Smart and William Balke), Romantic Madness (Lord Byron and Percy Bysshe Shelley); the influece of post-Kantian philosphy on Romantic art; the reality of the novel; medievalism in the artwork of 19th Centruy socialists; Ceremonial Magic (W. B. Yeats, Aleister Crowley and the erotics of form); 'How do we solve a problem like Ezra pound?' etc. £75 per h.
