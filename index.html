Le Salon

Maybe there are children to care for or a career which took you away from your academic studies.

Perhaps you studied Law, English, History or Philosophy, and wish you had studied History, Philosophy, Law or English..

Perhaps you've spent years garnering experise and want only an intelligent peer, to go with you through the unfamiliar.

L’Esprit

We shall talk, talk, talk; we shall dispute and wonder.

I will bring you texts, old and new, some of which you might know well, or half-remember, and some of which will be entirely unfamiliar, drawn from the peculiar store of my years of study.

Most of all we will seek, together, to make sense of the world, as we find it now, in the context of the truth and beauty of things past.

The salon is a French tradition, which began in the 17th century, where artists, writers, intellectuals and the otherwise curious met in private homes to talk about literature and philosophy and to enjoy good conversation. My version of the salon continues this tradition in the modern world.

La spécialité de la maison

I will guide you through the literature, history, philosophy and theology of the Anglophone World (and beyond it, when that's required). The course begins with the earliest account of Britain (Pytheas of Massalia) and works onwards to the present, at your own pace. (Though, of course, we can begin whenever you'd be most interested in beginning.)

Topics might include:

  • Post-Roman Anglo-Saxon poetry
  • The Viking invasion and the making of England
  • The tradition of courtly verse in Mediaeval Scots
  • Chaucer and the Arthurian legends
  • The Renaissance in various forms
  • Early-modern drama (Shakespeare & Co.)
  • The Reformation and the English Civil Wars
  • John Milton, Thomas Hobbes and the 17th Century crisis of free speech
  • Neoclassicism, Jonathan Swift, Alexander Pope
  • David Hume and the breakdown of the Enlightenment mind
  • The invention of Gothic; Romantic madness (Byron, Shelley)
  • Ceremonial Magic (Yeats, Crowley, and the erotics of form)
  • 'How do we solve a problem like Ezra Pound?'
